# Webinar 1: A Day in the Life of a Software Engineer

Date: February 16, 2026

## Overview

This session walks through what a software engineer actually does on a typical day. Not the Instagram version. Not the "10x developer coding at 2 a.m." version. The real version, based on my experience leading engineering projects at a major tech company and conversations with engineers across the industry.

If you are considering a career transition into software engineering, this session gives you the unfiltered picture so you can decide whether this work matches what you actually enjoy doing.


## What We Covered

Morning block (9:00-12:00)
- Standup: 15 minutes, each person shares what they did yesterday, what they are doing today, and any blockers. The real purpose is surfacing dependencies early, not status reporting.
- Code review: most senior engineers spend 30-60 minutes daily reviewing other people's code. This is not optional side work. It is a core part of the job and how teams maintain quality.
- Deep work: the remaining morning block is for writing code, debugging, or designing solutions. Protecting this block from meetings is something every experienced engineer learns to do.

Afternoon block (13:00-17:00)
- Design discussion or sprint planning: 1-2 hours depending on the day. These meetings involve whiteboarding architecture, debating tradeoffs, and aligning on technical approach.
- Debugging and investigation: 15.0-30.0% of an engineer's week goes to investigating production issues, reading logs, and tracking down bugs. This is the part most bootcamps do not prepare you for.
- Documentation: writing design docs, updating runbooks, and documenting decisions. Less glamorous than coding but critical for team velocity.

The parts nobody mentions:
- Context switching: you will be interrupted. Slack messages, urgent bugs, questions from other teams. Managing attention is an essential skill.
- Meetings: mid-level engineers average 6-10 hours of meetings per week. Senior engineers often have more. The "just code all day" fantasy does not match reality at most companies.
- On-call: many teams rotate on-call responsibility. You carry a pager (usually PagerDuty or Opsgenie) and respond to production alerts, sometimes at 3 a.m.


## Q&A Highlights

Q: How much of the day is actually writing code?
A: At most companies, individual contributors spend 40.0-60.0% of their time writing code. The rest is design, review, meetings, and communication. As you get more senior, the coding percentage typically decreases.

Q: What tools do you use daily?
A: IDE (VS Code or JetBrains), Git, Jira or Linear for task tracking, Slack for communication, CI/CD pipelines (GitHub Actions, Jenkins), monitoring dashboards (Datadog, Grafana), and a terminal. The specific tools vary by company but the categories are consistent.

Q: Is the transition from another field realistic?
A: Yes, but it takes deliberate preparation. The engineers i have seen make successful transitions typically spent 6-12 months building fundamentals, completed 2-3 substantial projects, and targeted their job search toward teams where their domain expertise added value.

Q: What surprised you most about the job?
A: How much of software engineering is communication. Writing clear commit messages, explaining your design decisions in a doc, giving constructive code review feedback, and articulating tradeoffs in a meeting are skills that matter as much as writing clean code.
