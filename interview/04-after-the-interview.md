# After the Interview

The interview is over, but the process is not. What you do in the days and weeks after your onsite rounds can meaningfully impact your outcome. I found consistent patterns across 100+ sources about how candidates handle this phase, and the gap between those who handle it well and those who don't is significant.

## Immediately After the Interview

Send a thank-you note within 24 hours. A short, specific email to your recruiter (who can forward to interviewers) works better than generic messages. Reference something concrete from each conversation: a technical problem you enjoyed discussing, a system design tradeoff that made you think, or a team project that excited you. Keep it under five sentences per interviewer.

Write down everything you remember. Before the details fade, document: what questions were asked, how you answered, where you felt strong, where you struggled, and any follow-up items. This serves two purposes: it helps you prepare for future interviews at other companies, and it gives you material if you need to follow up on anything.

Do not immediately start second-guessing. Post-interview anxiety is universal. I found that candidates' self-assessments right after an interview are poorly calibrated. People who thought they bombed get offers. People who thought they aced it get rejected. The correlation between how you feel leaving and the actual outcome is weak.

## The Waiting Period

Most companies take 3-10 business days after the onsite to make a decision. During this time:

It is acceptable to check in with your recruiter after 5-7 business days if you haven't heard anything. A simple "I wanted to check in on the timeline" message is appropriate. Do not send more than one follow-up per week.

If you have competing deadlines, communicate them. Telling your recruiter "I have an offer deadline from another company on [date]" is standard practice and often accelerates the process. Companies routinely expedite decisions when they know a candidate has alternatives. Do not fabricate competing offers; recruiters talk to each other more than candidates realize.

Use the waiting period to continue interviewing elsewhere. The best negotiating position comes from having multiple options. I consistently found that candidates with two or more offers negotiated 15.0-30.0% higher total compensation than those with a single offer.

## Understanding the Decision Process

At most large tech companies, the decision is not made by a single person. Here is what typically happens behind the scenes:

Each interviewer submits independent written feedback with a recommendation (strong hire, hire, lean hire, lean no hire, no hire). At companies like Google, a hiring committee reviews all feedback together. At Amazon, the Bar Raiser has special weight. At Meta, a hiring committee makes the call within a week.

A mixed signal (some hire, some no hire) does not automatically mean rejection. Hiring committees weigh the strength of positive signals against the concerns raised. A "strong hire" from one interviewer can outweigh a "lean no hire" from another, especially if the concern was about a skill that can be developed.

Recruiter tone during follow-up calls is a signal but not a guarantee. If they ask about your competing offers, timeline preferences, or team interests, the odds are favorable. If they are vague and noncommittal, prepare for either outcome.

## If You Get an Offer

Do not accept immediately, even if you are excited. Standard practice is to thank the recruiter, express enthusiasm, and ask for the details in writing. You typically have 1-2 weeks to decide, sometimes extendable to 3-4 weeks if you have other processes in flight.

Review the full compensation package. Total comp for software engineers includes base salary, equity (RSUs or stock options), sign-on bonus, annual bonus target, and benefits. A higher base with lower equity might be worse over four years than the reverse. Model it out.

Negotiate. The data overwhelmingly supports negotiation. I found that 70.0%+ of initial SWE offers at big tech companies have room for improvement. Common negotiation levers:

- Base salary: typically 5.0-15.0% increase is achievable
- Sign-on bonus: often the easiest lever to move; $10K-$50K additions are common
- Equity: can sometimes be increased by 10.0-20.0%, especially with competing offers
- Start date: flexibility here can affect sign-on bonus vesting
- Level: if you are borderline between levels, pushing for the higher level has the biggest long-term impact on compensation trajectory

How to negotiate effectively:
1. Get the offer in writing before discussing numbers
2. Express genuine enthusiasm for the role and team
3. Present your case with data (competing offers, market rates from levels.fyi, your unique qualifications)
4. Let the recruiter come back with a counter rather than making incremental asks
5. Know your walk-away number before the conversation starts
6. Be professional and collaborative, never adversarial

What not to do:
- Do not lie about competing offers (recruiters verify)
- Do not negotiate base salary at the expense of equity (equity typically grows faster)
- Do not issue ultimatums unless you are genuinely prepared to walk
- Do not negotiate via email when a phone call would be more effective

## If You Get Rejected

Rejection stings, but it is a normal part of the process. Here is what I found about handling it productively:

Ask for feedback. Not all companies provide it, but about 40.0% will share general areas for improvement if you ask politely. Even vague feedback like "system design could be stronger" gives you direction.

You can often reapply. Most companies have a cooldown period of 6-12 months before you can interview again for the same role. Some have shorter windows for different teams. Your recruiter can usually tell you the specific policy.

Analyze what went wrong honestly. Was it a specific technical gap (system design, dynamic programming), a communication issue, or just a bad day? Focus your preparation on the actual gap, not on grinding more of what you already know.

Do not take it personally. False negative rates in tech interviews are high. Studies suggest that 20.0-30.0% of candidates who are rejected would have been successful employees. The interview format tests a narrow slice of engineering ability under artificial conditions.

## Evaluating Multiple Offers

If you are fortunate enough to have multiple offers, evaluate them across these dimensions:

Compensation: Model total comp over 4 years, not just year one. Equity vesting schedules vary (Google backloads, Amazon backloads heavily, Meta is roughly even). Factor in refreshers.

Team and manager: The team you join matters more than the company name. Ask to speak with potential teammates during the decision period. A strong manager at a less prestigious company often beats a weak manager at a household name.

Growth trajectory: Where does this role lead in 2-3 years? What is the promotion velocity on this team? What skills will you develop?

Technical challenge: Will you work on interesting problems? Will you learn new technologies? Will you be bored in six months?

Work-life balance: Ask about on-call rotations, deploy frequency, expected hours, and remote/hybrid policies. These vary enormously even within the same company.

Location and logistics: Commute time, cost of living, relocation support, and remote work flexibility all affect your daily quality of life.

## The First 90 Days

Once you accept and start, the interview mindset should shift to a delivery mindset. A few patterns I found from engineers who ramped up quickly:

- Ship something small in the first two weeks, even if it is a one-line bug fix
- Ask questions aggressively in the first month; nobody expects you to know everything
- Find a team member who can be an informal mentor or onboarding buddy
- Understand the codebase before trying to improve it
- Document what you learn; your fresh perspective is valuable to the team

## Key Takeaway

The post-interview phase is where leverage is created or lost. Candidates who negotiate effectively, evaluate offers holistically, and handle rejection with a growth mindset end up in better positions over the long run. Treat every interview, regardless of outcome, as data that makes your next one better.
