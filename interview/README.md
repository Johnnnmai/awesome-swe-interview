# Interview Preparation: Software Engineering

This section covers everything I found about software engineering interviews after analyzing data from 1,500+ SWE job descriptions, 100+ Reddit discussions, 60+ Hacker News threads, 50+ Twitter/X posts, 35+ personal blogs, 20 YouTube videos, and 700+ URLs across 100+ domains.

The SWE interview landscape in 2025-2026 is intense. Companies are raising the bar, AI tools are reshaping how candidates prepare and perform, and the gap between what schools teach and what interviews test continues to widen. I organized my findings into five sections that follow the natural arc of preparing for, going through, and learning from software engineering interviews.

## What This Section Covers

[Interview Process](01-interview-process.md)
A breakdown of how companies structure their SWE hiring pipelines. I analyzed ~120 job postings (roughly 8.0% of the dataset) that included structured interview details across 60+ unique companies. This section maps the typical 5-7 step process from recruiter screen to offer, with real examples from Google, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft.

[Interview Questions](questions/)
The core of interview preparation. I organized questions into six categories based on what actually shows up in interviews:
- [Data Structures and Algorithms](questions/01-theory.md) - the foundational knowledge every SWE candidate needs
- [Coding Challenges and Patterns](questions/02-coding.md) - LeetCode-style patterns that repeat across companies
- [System Design](questions/03-system-design.md) - architecture questions for mid-to-senior roles
- [Object-Oriented Design](questions/04-oop-design.md) - class design and design pattern questions
- [Behavioral Questions](questions/05-behavioral.md) - adapted for software engineers, not generic
- [Take-Home Assignments](questions/06-home-assignments.md) - the growing trend of async evaluation

[Getting Hired](03-get-hired.md)
What actually separates candidates who get offers from those who don't. Based on 100+ sources including interviewer blog posts, hiring manager AMAs, and post-interview debriefs. Covers the 8-12 week preparation timeline, compensation ranges, portfolio strategy, and the most common mistakes I found candidates making.

[After the Interview](04-after-the-interview.md)
What to do once the interview is over: follow-up timing, negotiation tactics, handling rejections, and how to evaluate competing offers. This is where many candidates leave money on the table or make decisions they regret.

[Interview Trends](05-trends.md)
How SWE interviews are changing in 2026. AI coding assistants in interviews, the return of in-person rounds, AI proctoring, and whether LeetCode-style interviews are finally being replaced (spoiler: not yet, but the cracks are showing).

## How to Use This Guide

If you have 12 weeks before your first interview, start with the preparation timeline in Getting Hired, then work through the question categories systematically.

If you have an interview next week, go straight to the Interview Process section to understand what to expect, then focus on the question categories most relevant to your target company and level.

If you just finished an interview and are waiting to hear back, read After the Interview for negotiation preparation and next steps.

## A Note on the Data

I collected this data between late 2025 and mid-2026. The SWE job market moves fast. Compensation numbers, company processes, and hiring trends shift quarter to quarter. Use the specific numbers as directional guidance, not gospel. The patterns and preparation strategies hold up much longer than any specific data point.

The sources span the full spectrum: official company career pages, engineering blogs, Blind posts, Reddit threads from r/cscareerquestions and r/leetcode, Hacker News hiring threads, conference talks, YouTube mock interview channels, and personal blogs from engineers who recently went through the process. I weighted insights from people who hire over people who speculate about hiring.
